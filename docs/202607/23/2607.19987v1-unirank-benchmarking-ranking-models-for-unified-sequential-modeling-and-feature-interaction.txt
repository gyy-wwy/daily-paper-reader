## Abstract 

Ranking is a core stage in online advertising and recommender systems. Modern ranking models increasingly unify sequential modeling and feature interaction, yet many advances rely on pro-prietary data, closed implementations, and large-scale industrial infrastructure. This setting limits reproducible comparison and hin-ders academic study of scaling laws, long-sequence modeling, and multi-task ranking. To address these limitations, this paper pro-poses UniRank , an open benchmark for ranking models that unify sequential modeling and feature interaction. UniRank uses chrono-logical pointwise autoregressive supervision, standardizes evalu-ation across feedback tasks, and provides a PyTorch toolkit with Distributed Data Parallel training, operator optimization, mixed-precision training, attention optimization, and other efficiency tech-niques that reduce hardware requirements. We benchmark 15 repre-sentative unified ranking models on five large-scale public datasets from short-video, advertising, and e-commerce platforms, with the largest dataset containing over 700 million instances and the longest behavior sequence exceeding 10 5 interactions. UniRank provides a reproducible basis for comparing unified ranking mod-els, studying scaling laws under limited compute, and narrowing the gap between academic and industrial ranking research. We believe UniRank benefits researchers, practitioners, and beginners through reproducible experiments, production-oriented evaluation, and accessible implementations. Code and data are on GitHub.

## 1 Introduction 

Recommender systems drive user engagement and business value in e-commerce [ 13 , 89 ], short-video streaming [ 14 ], and online adver-tising [ 6 , 71 ]. Under large item corpora and strict latency, industrial systems use retrieval [8 , 29 ], pre-ranking [ 15 , 73 ], ranking [ 17 , 37 ], and re-ranking [1, 55]. 

> ∗Corresponding author

Within this pipeline, the ranking model is the central decision component [ 17 ]. Given heterogeneous multi-field features, includ-ing user profiles, item attributes, device types, request times, and historical behaviors, among others, it estimates the probabilities of feedback events such as clicks and conversions [ 25 , 37 , 93 ]. Rank-ing models directly affect user experience and platform revenue. Consequently, even small improvements in offline metrics such as the area under the ROC curve (AUC) can correspond to mean-ingful business gains [ 6]. Ranking model design and optimization therefore remain important in both academia and industry [ 13 , 89 ]. Ranking models evolve toward more expressive feature represen-tations and user intent signals. This progression yields three broad methodological families: (1) The Shallow Learning Method uses Lo-gistic Regression [ 61 ] and factorization-based models [ 27 , 60 , 66 ]. These models are efficient but have limited capacity and rely on manual feature engineering. (2) The Deep Feature Interaction Method 

embeds sparse categorical features and replaces manual crosses with neural interaction modules. Representative models use MLPs, compressed interaction networks, cross networks, or quadratic neurons to learn nonlinear high-order feature combinations, as in DeepFM [ 17 ], xDeepFM [ 37 ], DCNv2 [ 71 ], and QNN [ 31 ]. (3) The Se-quence Modeling Method captures temporal changes in user interest. Models such as DIN [ 89 ] and DIEN [ 13 ] use target-aware attention or recurrent networks to aggregate histories for the target item. To improve performance, deployed ranking models [ 49 , 83 ] may inte-grate multiple DCNv2, MLP, and DIN modules. Such non-unified designs introduce fine-grained operators and execution boundaries that limit parallel scaling [92]. Inspired by large language models [88, 92], current research in-creasingly adopts token-based unified architectures that combine sequential modeling with feature interaction, improve Model FLOPs Utilization (MFU), and scale more effectively with sequence length and model capacity. These models follow either stacked designs [ 16 ,18 , 26 , 70 , 79 , 84 , 92 ], which perform sequential modeling before feature interaction, or layer-wise designs [ 3 , 11 , 23 , 24 , 32 , 41 , 85 , 90 ], which integrate both operations within each layer. Together, they provide a unified foundation for studying how sequential model-ing and feature interaction affect scaling laws under a consistent ranking protocol. arXiv:2607.19987v1 [cs.IR] 22 Jul 2026 Conference acronym ’XX, June 03–05, 2018, Woodstock, NY Anonymous et al. 

Despite this progress, a substantial gap remains between aca-demic and industrial research. Many strong ranking models, large-scale datasets, and training systems remain private. Academic researchers therefore lack resources to study scaling laws, long-sequence behavior, and task trade-offs under realistic conditions, hindering reproducibility and comparison. Existing studies and toolkits improve reproducible recommen-dation evaluation, including FuxiCTR [ 25 , 93 ], Scenario-Wise Rec [ 34 ], DeepCTR [ 63 ], RecBole [ 87 ], and MMLRec [ 80 ]. FuxiCTR and DeepCTR focus on single-task CTR prediction, RecBole covers general and sequential recommendation, and Scenario-Wise Rec and MMLRec emphasize multi-scenario or multi-task learning. However, these frameworks provide limited support for industrial-scale long-sequence modeling. They do not jointly standardize chronological data partitions, behavior sequence construction, model capacity, and evaluation across feedback tasks. Consequently, they provide limited evidence on how sequential modeling and feature interaction scale across clicks, likes, conversions, and other signals. To address these issues, we propose the Unified Ranking Model Benchmark ( UniRank ) for ranking models with unified sequen-tial modeling and feature interaction. UniRank evaluates 15 repre-sentative models on five large-scale public datasets from short-video, advertising, and e-commerce platforms. It also provides handbook-style controlled experiments on tokenization, activation functions, architectural enhancements, optimizers, sequence length, and model scale. These experiments standardize architectural stud-ies and reduce the cost of rebuilding datasets, implementations, and ablations. This work serves several groups of readers: 

• Researchers : UniRank provides accessible open-source code and processed datasets for reproducible model development. 

• Practitioners : UniRank provides acceleration components, sup-ports multi-feedback tasks, and offers controlled production-oriented recipes for practical validation. 

• Beginners : The repository serves as an educational resource with accessible PyTorch implementations that help readers un-derstand modern unified ranking architectures. In summary, our work makes the following main contributions: 

• We propose UniRank , the first open benchmark dedicated to ranking architectures that unify sequential modeling and feature interaction, enabling systematic comparison under standardized data, optimization, and efficiency settings. 

• We integrate DDP, operator, mixed-precision, and attention opti-mizations to reduce training hardware costs. 

• We release code, configurations, and results for 15 models on five public datasets, with up to 700 million instances and behavior sequences over 10 5 events. 

## 2 Ranking Models 2.1 Input Features and Modeling Paradigms 

Ranking models estimate target-item feedback probabilities. Uni-Rank represents each impression with non-sequential features F ,behavior sequences S, and target features V:

F = {𝑓 1, . . . , 𝑓 𝑚 }, S = (𝑠 1, . . . , 𝑠 𝐿 ), V = {𝑣 1, . . . , 𝑣 𝑛 }, (1) where F contains non-sequential user and context fields, such as 

user ID, age group, membership level, device type, request time, and traffic source . S is the chronological behavior sequence and includes 

historical item-side features and action types, such as category, creator or seller, brand, and other available metadata . V contains the current target-item features. During training, the latest behavior in each instance defines the target and feedback label, while S contains only preceding behaviors. The following three paradigms progressively incorporate S into prediction. 

2.1.1 Traditional Non-Sequential Modeling Paradigm . This paradigm models interactions between non-sequential heteroge-neous features and target features: 

ˆ𝑦 = 𝜙 ns (F , V) . (2) This formulation covers shallow and deep subnetworks that learn feature crosses from F and V without modeling S. DeepFM [ 17 ]and DCNv2 [ 71 ] are representative examples. Because they omit temporal dependencies, UniRank treats them as conventional base-lines outside the unified benchmark. 

2.1.2 Stacked Unified Interaction . This paradigm arranges se-quence aggregation and feature interaction as consecutive stages. An aggregation operator first produces a sequence representation, and a downstream network then combines it with F and V:

𝐻 S = 𝑔 agg (S , V) , ˆ𝑦 = 𝜙 stack (F , V, 𝐻 S ). (3) where 𝐻 S is the target-aware sequence representation, 𝑔 agg aggre-gates S conditioned on V, and 𝜙 stack is the downstream interac-tion and prediction network. DIN [ 89 ] provides an early example through target-aware pooling, but it does not use a token-based unified architecture and is not included in the benchmark. Uni-Rank instead evaluates token-based models such as HiFormer [ 16 ], RankMixer [92], and UniMixer [18]. 

2.1.3 Layer-wise Unified Interaction . This paradigm ex-changes information between sequential and non-sequential representations within every interaction layer: 

(𝐻 (𝑙 +1)S , 𝐻 (𝑙 +1)F,V ) = 𝑔 (𝑙 ) (𝐻 (𝑙 )S , 𝐻 (𝑙 )F,V ), ˆ𝑦 = 𝜙 out (𝐻 (𝐿 ) ). The function 𝑔 (𝑙 ) performs unified interaction within layer 𝑙 , 𝐻 (𝐿 ) is the final representation, and 𝜙 out maps it to ˆ𝑦 . Unlike stacked architectures, this formulation preserves behavior, field, and target representations across layers and supports iterative information exchange. OneTrans [ 85 ], To-kenFormer [ 90 ], and UltraHSTU [ 11 ] instantiate this paradigm with different attention and mixing operators.

## 2.3 Representative Models 

2.3.1 Stacked Unified Interaction . This paradigm arranges se-quential modeling and feature interaction as consecutive modules. The sequence module first encodes the behavioral history, and the feature interaction module then combines this representation with user, target-item, and context features. 

• HiFormer [16 ] pools the behavior history with target atten-tion, tokenizes heterogeneous fields and task queries, and applies hierarchical HiFormer blocks to the stacked tokens. 

• RankMixer [92 ] pools the history with target attention, tok-enizes concatenated ranking fields into chunks, and alternates parameter-free Token Mixing with per-token feed-forward net-works. 

• Zenith [84 ] separately embeds user and item identifiers, pools historical item attributes with target attention, tokenizes context and target attributes, and applies Zenith interaction blocks. 

• TokenMixer [ 26 ] combines target-aware sequence summaries with global and group tokens, then applies reversible token mix-ing and per-token feed-forward networks. 

• UniMixer [18 ] flattens histories with user and context fields, then chunk-tokenizes them for UniMixing-Lite blocks. 

• HeMix [70 ] combines fixed and feature-conditioned queries with low-rank token mixing over recent and earlier histories. 

• SSR [ 79 ] uses target-attention sequence pooling and multi-view competitive sparse filters with dense fusion. 

2.3.2 Layer-wise Unified Interaction . This paradigm integrates sequential and non-sequential representations throughout every network layer. 

• OneTrans [ 85 ] injects sequence and partitioned non-sequential tokens into every block, using mixed attention, a shared sequence FFN, and token-specific FFNs. 

• HyFormer [24 ] encodes the sequence per layer, decodes global queries, and enriches them with non-sequential tokens. 

• MixFormer [ 23 ] mixes non-sequential queries, uses cross-attention to retrieve information from the updated sequence, and fuses the resulting representations. 

• INFNet [ 32 ] organizes categorical, sequential, and task tokens around a hub for layer-wise aggregation and broadcasting. 

• EST [41 ] uses cross-attention to sequence representations and feature-specific feed-forward layers. 

• TokenFormer [ 90 ] represents fields, histories, and targets as tokens with full interaction and sliding-window Flex Attention. 

• LONGER [3] encodes local groups with inner Transformers and joins compressed long-term history with recent queries. 

• UltraHSTU [11 ] extends HSTU-style sequential transduc-tion [ 82 ] with hybrid attention that combines sparse and full attention in each interaction layer. Conference acronym ’XX, June 03–05, 2018, Woodstock, NY Anonymous et al.

## 3 Benchmarking Ranking Models 3.1 Reproducibility Analysis 

Ranking models receive substantial research attention, but many advances rely on private data, closed implementations, and propri-etary training infrastructure. This dependence limits reproducibility and obscures whether gains arise from model architecture, data processing, training pipelines, or engineering optimization. Differ-ent public datasets and reproduction pipelines can even reverse model rankings in small-scale experiments. We therefore define six requirements for reproducible and fair ranking model research. 

• Unified data pipeline. Studies use consistent raw-log filtering, feature extraction, categorical encoding, sequence construction, target alignment, chronological splitting, truncation, and padding. This creates matched samples and prevents future leakage from confounding comparisons. 

• Unified task and metric definitions. Each feedback task uses consistent labels, sample eligibility rules, loss functions, metric implementations, and aggregation procedures. This ensures that architectural differences, not task formulation, drive measured results. 

• Unified model source code. Baselines use complete, verifiable source code and consistent interfaces. This avoids confusing implementation differences with architecture. 

• Unified hyperparameters. Shared capacity parameters such as network depth, token count, token dimension, embedding dimension, and sequence length should be aligned as closely as possible across baselines. This prevents capacity mismatches from driving apparent model gains, while architecture-specific parameters vary only for defining operations. 

• Unified training pipeline. Baselines share data loading, train-ing, validation, and testing. The pipeline also controls optimizers, learning rates, effective batch size, accumulation, initialization, epochs, and task weighting so that training choices do not mas-querade as architectural improvements. 

• Unified efficiency optimization. Studies align and disclose distributed training, precision, attention kernels, operator com-pilation, checkpointing, hardware, and software configurations. This makes performance and resource use comparable under the same systems setting. Table 1 shows that many studies do not satisfy all six require-ments. 3.2.2 Data Splitting . Figure 3 compares a randomized user-disjoint split with UniRank’s chronological split. The user-disjoint protocol assigns each user and their entire interaction history ex-clusively to one data partition. In fact, this protocol is better suited to cold-start evaluation, as it primarily measures generalization to unseen users rather than future interactions from returning users. In addition, the partitions may cover overlapping calendar periods, allowing training interactions to occur after validation or test interactions from other users. Such temporal inversion exposes the model to future popularity patterns and platform trends, making offline evaluation less representative of deployment. UniRank instead orders all interactions chronologically and de-termines global temporal cutoffs that approximately allocate the earliest 80% of interactions to training, the following 10% to valida-tion, and the most recent 10% to testing. Within each user trajectory, each target interaction is constructed using only preceding behav-ior. The validation period supports model configuration tuning, while the test period evaluates performance on the latest interac-tions. This protocol emulates past-to-future prediction by training on users’ earlier interactions and evaluating their subsequent inter-actions, while also assessing robustness to shifts in user interests over time. 

3.2.3 Data Preprocessing . UniRank groups features into four cat-egories: user features describe persistent attributes, context features describe the current request, sequence features store chronological behavioral items, and action features encode historical feedback. Values below the categorical frequency threshold map to a shared out-of-vocabulary (OOV) index. Masked average pooling aggre-gates multi-valued features without padding, and bucketization converts dense numerical features into categorical indices. All mod-els share this preprocessing interface. 

3.2.4 Evaluation Metrics . The benchmark reports two widely used metrics, binary Logloss and AUC, for every task [ 25 , 63 , 93 ]. Lower Logloss indicates better probabilistic calibration, whereas higher AUC indicates better global discrimination.

3.2.6 Training and Reproducibility . UniRank uses Py-Torch [ 54 ] because its imperative execution, modular interfaces, and transparent debugging simplify development and reproduction. The default batch size is 8192 per GPU. If an experiment encounters an out-of-memory error, we reduce the per-GPU batch size linearly and increase gradient accumulation by the same factor to preserve the effective batch size. Each model trains for one epoch [ 86 ] with separate sparse and dense optimizers, consistent with production practice. Adagrad optimizes sparse embeddings at a default learning rate of 0.05 , while AdamW optimizes dense parameters at 0.0001 .All experiments run on NVIDIA H20 GPUs. The main benchmark fixes the embedding dimension at 16, network depth at three layers, and token dimension at 256. All interaction blocks use pre-norm for stable optimization. ## 3.4 Efficiency Analysis 

Figure 5 compares total parameters and one-epoch training time under the same NVIDIA H20 GPUs, per-GPU batch size, sequence length, and precision policy. Zenith [84] has a substantially larger parameter footprint because it expands user and item ID embed-dings and retains both IDs as independent tokens. Most of this ca-pacity lies in sparse embedding tables, while target-aware pooling compresses the behavior sequence before interaction. Its training time therefore does not increase proportionally with parameter count. TokenFormer [ 90 ] and UltraHSTU [ 11 ] retain sequence to-kens across interaction layers and apply structured sparse attention, which increases active computation. Flex Attention can further in-cur kernel-launch overhead on short sequences, so its training speed is not necessarily advantageous. Dedicated kernels and smaller sparse-attention windows may further improve throughput. Uni-Rank prioritizes fair model comparison and tunes each model by validation AUC rather than peak system throughput, leaving further system optimization beyond its scope. Overall, parameter count reflects representation capacity, whereas epoch time reflects active token computation. Both are necessary for evaluating practical efficiency. 

## 3.5 A Practical Handbook 

3.5.1 Tokenization Strategies .

• Chunk [92 ] flattens the fields, divides the resulting vector into contiguous chunks, and maps each chunk to one token. 

• Auto [19] allows each token projection to access all features. 

• Field [16 ] preserves field boundaries and independently maps each field to one token. 

• Random [5 ] fixes a random field permutation, divides the fields into balanced groups, and maps each group to one token. Table 4 shows that tokenization imposes an architectural inductive bias. Auto preserves a global view and learns cross-field dependen-cies directly. Field protects semantic boundaries and reduces inter-ference. Chunk and Random partition fields into multiple groups that promote diverse and complementary token representations, increasing their informational entropy [ 62 ]. Their effectiveness depends on whether interaction layers benefit from global access, semantic isolation, or controlled grouping.

3.5.2 Attention Activations. We compare the Softmax base-line [ 68 ] with unnormalized logits [ 89 ], ReLU [ 78 ], SoftPlus [ 28 ], SiLU [ 12 ], GeLU [ 21 ], Sigmoid [ 28 ], and Mish [ 48 ]. Table 5 shows that smooth signed activations provide the most robust improve-ments. GeLU achieves the highest average AUC on both KuaiRand and MerRec, improving the baseline from 0.8548 to 0.8599 and from 0.8051 to 0.8089, respectively, while SiLU performs similarly. This result suggests that preserving both positive and negative atten-tion responses is more effective than enforcing purely competitive or non-negative aggregation. In contrast, SoftPlus substantially degrades KuaiRand, indicating that accumulating weak positive weights may introduce behavioral noise. The optimal choice also varies across tasks. Sigmoid performs best for Like on both datasets, whereas unnormalized logits mainly benefit Click and Long View. Overall, GeLU and SiLU are strong performance-oriented defaults, while Softmax remains attractive when fused-kernel efficiency is important [9]. 

3.5.3 Architectural Enhancements. 

• SpecT [76] adds learnable boundary tokens as attention sinks. 

• AttGate [58] applies a learned sigmoid gate after attention. 

• RoPE [65 ] rotates sequence queries and keys to encode position. 

• QKNorm [22 ] normalizes queries and keys before their dot prod-uct. 

• GNorm [ 75 ] uses heterogeneous LayerNorm: sequence tokens share one, while non-sequential tokens use individual ones. 

• AttRes [20 ] adds residual attention logits across adjacent layers. 

• Tau learns a separate attention scale for each head. Table 6 shows that these enhancements have substantially differ-ent cross-dataset robustness. AttGate is the only component that improves every task on both datasets, raising the average AUC to 0.8636 on KuaiRand and 0.8150 on MerRec. It therefore provides the safest general-purpose enhancement by suppressing target-irrelevant attention updates. QKNorm and GNorm perform best on KuaiRand, but GNorm degrades MerRec, suggesting that hetero-geneous normalization may remove useful magnitude differences between token groups. RoPE provides larger gains on MerRec, indicating that chronological order has dataset-dependent impor-tance. SpecT mainly benefits KuaiRand, whereas Tau reduces its average AUC. These results show that most enhancements align with dataset-specific characteristics rather than providing universal gains.

## 4 Related Work 

Ranking Models. Ranking models have evolved through four broad stages: shallow feature interaction, deep feature interaction, sequence modeling, and unified modeling. Early shallow methods assign weights to individual features or capture low-order interac-tions through factorization-based formulations [ 27 , 51 , 60 , 61 , 77 ]. Deep feature interaction methods subsequently embed sparse cat-egorical features and replace manual feature crosses with neu-ral interaction modules. Some studies learn implicit interactions through multilayer perceptrons or product transformations, while others explicitly construct high-order interactions through cross networks, self-attention, graph propagation, or polynomial func-tions [ 17 , 30 , 31 , 35 –37 , 59 , 64 , 71 , 72 ]. Further studies reduce manual design through automatic interaction selection, feature grouping, and architecture search [ 10 , 38 , 39 , 47 , 91 ]. Despite their effective-ness, these methods primarily treat each impression as an isolated set of non-sequential fields, making it difficult to capture the tem-poral evolution of user interests from behavior histories. Sequence modeling methods address this limitation by aggregat-ing behavior histories according to the target item and tracking the temporal evolution of user interest [ 13 , 29 , 89 ]. Multi-task meth-ods further share information across clicks, conversions, and other feedback signals through shared representations, expert routing, and task-specific parameters [ 4 , 45 , 46 , 52 ]. However, these systems commonly combine separate sequence modules, feature interac-tion modules, and task towers, resulting in heterogeneous architec-tures in which sequence modeling and feature interaction remain loosely coupled. Recent unified ranking methods instead repre-sent behavior sequences, feature fields, targets, and task queries through a common token interface. Some studies perform sequence modeling before feature interaction [ 16 , 18 , 26 , 70 , 79 , 84 , 92 ], whereas others integrate both operations within each interaction layer [ 3 , 11 , 23 , 24 , 32 , 41 , 85 , 90 ]. By replacing heterogeneous fine-grained modules with a common computational interface, this pro-gression improves MFU and yields more favorable scaling curves as sequence length and model capacity increase [88, 92]. 

Benchmarking. Existing recommendation benchmarks provide complementary forms of standardization. FuxiCTR [ 25 , 93 ] and DeepCTR [ 63 ] focus on single-task CTR models evaluated on conventional tabular datasets, while RecBole [ 87 ] supports a broader range of general and sequential recommendation. Scenario-Wise Rec [ 34 ] and MMLRec [ 80 ] further extend evaluation to multi-scenario or multi-task prediction, but offer limited sup-port for industrial-scale behavioral sequences and token-based unified architectures. Overall, these benchmarks do not jointly standardize chronological sample construction, multi-task supervi-sion, capacity-controlled model comparison, production-oriented training protocols, and efficiency optimization. To the best of our knowledge, UniRank is the first benchmark specifically designed UniRank: Benchmarking Ranking Models for Unified Sequential Modeling and Feature Interaction Conference acronym ’XX, June 03–05, 2018, Woodstock, NY 

for token-based unified ranking models. It integrates large-scale public datasets, long behavior sequences, multi-task supervision, controlled experimental configurations, and optimized training pipelines within a unified framework. 

## 5 Conclusion and Future Work 

This paper proposed UniRank, an open benchmark for ranking models with unified sequential modeling and feature interaction. UniRank provided standardized evaluation protocols, an optimized PyTorch toolkit, and five large-scale public datasets from diverse platforms. Its chronological pointwise autoregressive paradigm and acceleration components supported reproducible comparison of modern ranking architectures and enabled practical studies of scaling laws. We reported results for 15 unified ranking models and established a foundation for more open research on modern recommender systems. Future versions will extend UniRank to mul-timodal ranking models and datasets such as TaoBao-MM [ 74 ] and MicroLens [ 50 ]. We will also incorporate larger datasets, including MerRec-1B [33], RecFlow (1B) [42], and VK-LSVD (40B) [57].
